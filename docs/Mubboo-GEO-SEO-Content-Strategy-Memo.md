## 1. The Big Shift: Keywords Are Dead, Scenarios Are King

### What Changed

In the SEO era, users typed short keywords: "London hotels." Optimization meant stuffing those words everywhere.

In the GEO era, users talk to AI in full sentences: "I'm taking my elderly parents to London next month. They can't walk long distances. Where should we stay that's flat, close to the Tube, and under £150 a night?"

AI doesn't match keywords — it matches scenarios. The content that covers the most real-life travel situations wins the most AI referrals.

### The "Salt in Water" Principle

Never list product features or affiliate advantages in isolation. Instead, dissolve them naturally into the travel scenario being described.

**Mechanical (avoid):**
> "Trip.com offers 1.7 million hotels with 5.5% commission and 30-day cookies."

**Natural (aim for):**
> "South Kensington works beautifully for families with older parents — the streets are flat, three Tube stations have step-free access within a 5-minute walk, and there's a pharmacy on every other corner. Hotels in this area average around £150 per night, and most offer free cancellation if plans change."

The product advantage (price, free cancellation) is there, but it serves the reader's scenario rather than advertising itself.

### Scenario Thinking — A Menu, Not a Checklist

Different articles call for different scenarios. Here's a pool to draw from — pick whichever ones naturally fit the destination and page type:

**Travel companion scenarios:**
- Elderly parents or grandparents (mobility, medical access, comfort)
- Young families with kids (safety, family rooms, activities, stroller-friendly)
- Solo travelers (safety at night, social neighborhoods, single-friendly dining)
- Couples / honeymoon (romance, views, special experiences)
- Groups of friends (nightlife proximity, multi-room options)

**Budget scenarios:**
- Backpackers / students (hostels, free attractions, transit passes)
- Mid-range comfort seekers (value-for-money sweet spots)
- Luxury travelers (premium experiences, exclusive access)

**Purpose scenarios:**
- Business travelers (convention centers, early breakfast, reliable Wi-Fi)
- Remote workers / digital nomads (apartment-style, kitchen, monthly rates)
- First-time visitors vs. repeat visitors (orientation needs differ)
- Transit / layover travelers (airport proximity, short-stay options)

**Situation scenarios:**
- Peak season vs. off-season visitors (pricing, crowds, availability)
- Travelers with dietary restrictions (food scene guidance)
- Travelers needing visa or entry guidance
- Travelers concerned about language barriers
- Travelers with accessibility needs

Not every article needs all of these. A Hotel City page for Tokyo might naturally emphasize first-time visitors, families, and business travelers. A flight route article for NYC to Miami might focus on weekend couples, spring breakers, and snowbirds. Let the destination guide the selection.

## 2. E-E-A-T: Experience Is the New Currency

### What Google (and AI) Reward in 2026

Google's March 2026 Core Update elevated **Experience** to the highest weight in E-E-A-T. Content must demonstrate that the author (or editorial team) has genuine knowledge — not just aggregated information.

**What this means for Mubboo:**

- Every recommendation should feel like it comes from someone who's been there or thoroughly researched as if they're going
- Use specific details: street names, walking distances in minutes, price ranges in local currency, names of nearby landmarks
- Include honest anti-recommendations: "Skip the tourist-trap restaurants on [Street X]" or "This area looks great on Instagram but it's noisy at night"
- Minimum 3-4 first-person "we" moments per article (Mubboo editorial voice)

### What Gets Penalized

- AI-generated content with no editorial layer or human judgment
- Generic advice that could apply to any destination ("something for everyone")
- Keyword stuffing, even subtle versions
- Fake urgency ("Book now before prices go up!")
- Empty authority claims ("According to experts...")

## 3. Link-Worthy Content: Earn Links, Don't Chase Them

### The Best Links Come to You

Instead of submitting to directories (we've done the basics), focus on creating content that other sites naturally want to reference:

**Data-driven assets:**
- Original price analysis with real API data (e.g., "Average flight costs from 10 US cities to London — monthly breakdown")
- Destination comparison tables with editorial verdicts
- Seasonal travel calendars with price and crowd data

**Utility assets:**
- Interactive tools (budget planners, visa requirement checkers, jet lag calculators)
- Downloadable resources (packing checklists, itinerary templates)

**Insight assets:**
- Contrarian takes backed by data ("Why you shouldn't visit [popular destination] in [popular month]")
- Deep-dive guides that become the definitive resource on a niche topic

These assets get linked by travel bloggers, forums, and media without us asking.

## 4. The Self-Test

After writing any article, run this mental check:

**Scenario match test:** Pick three realistic questions a traveler might ask an AI chatbot. Would our article be surfaced as the best answer?

Examples:
- "I'm taking my kids to [destination] for the first time — is it safe and what area should we stay?"
- "I have a business conference at [venue] — where's the most convenient hotel nearby?"
- "My parents want to visit [destination] but they have mobility issues — can they manage?"

If our content doesn't naturally answer at least 2-3 questions like these, it needs more scenario depth.

**Specificity test:** Does every recommendation include at least two of: a specific area/neighborhood, a price range, a time estimate (walking distance, transit time), or a practical tip?

**Voice test:** Does this read like advice from a knowledgeable friend, or like a brochure? If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it.

## 5. Summary

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| Scenarios over keywords | Cover real travel situations relevant to each destination |
| Salt in water | Weave product advantages into scenario context naturally |
| Diversity over templates | Let the destination and page type determine which scenarios matter |
| Experience over claims | Specific details, honest opinions, editorial judgment |
| Earn links, don't buy them | Create content worth referencing — data, tools, definitive guides |
| Self-test every article | "Would AI recommend this to a traveler describing their specific situation?" |
