When I started building Mubboo, the most common question I got was: "Why would you build separate sites for each country when you could just build one global platform?"

It's a fair question. The conventional wisdom in tech is to build once, deploy everywhere, and localise later. It's faster, cheaper, and simpler. It's also, I believe, fundamentally wrong for what we're trying to do.

Mubboo exists to help everyday consumers make better decisions across the key areas of their lives — shopping, travel, finance, local services, and everyday information. And the reality is that those decisions are deeply local. The best savings account in Australia is irrelevant in the UK. The top-rated restaurant in Auckland means nothing to someone in New York. The regulations governing consumer rights in Canada are different from those in New Zealand.

Global platforms treat localisation as translation. We treat it as the entire point.

## The problem with global-first platforms

Most consumer comparison and information platforms start with one market — usually the US — and then expand internationally by translating their content and swapping in local products where they can. The result is a site that feels generically international: technically accurate in places, but lacking the depth and context that makes information genuinely useful.

I've experienced this firsthand. As an Australian consumer, I've lost count of the times I've searched for product comparisons, financial advice, or travel information and ended up on a US-centric site that either didn't cover Australia at all or covered it with surface-level, clearly-templated content.

The prices were in USD. The regulations referenced were American. The brands recommended weren't available locally. The "local" version was clearly an afterthought — a thin layer of localisation painted over a global template.

This isn't a niche problem. Billions of consumers outside the US face this every day. And the issue isn't just inconvenience — it's about trust. When you can see that a platform doesn't really understand your market, you stop trusting its recommendations.

## What "local" actually means

When we say Mubboo is built for local markets, we mean something specific. It's not about translating content into different languages. It's not about swapping USD for AUD. It's about building each country's content from the ground up, with genuine understanding of that market.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

**Real local brands and retailers.** In Australia, we don't recommend products from retailers that don't ship there. We cover JB Hi-Fi, Kmart, Bunnings, and Officeworks — the actual stores Australians shop at. In the UK, it's Currys, Argos, John Lewis, and Tesco. Not a global list with a flag emoji stuck on top.

**Actual local prices.** Prices in Australian dollars, British pounds, New Zealand dollars — not converted from USD. Prices that reflect what consumers actually pay in that market, including local taxes and shipping.

**Local regulations and consumer rights.** Financial products in Australia are governed by ASIC and the National Consumer Credit Protection Act. In the UK, it's the FCA. In the US, it's the CFPB, the SEC, and a patchwork of state-level regulators. These differences aren't footnotes — they fundamentally change what products are available, how they work, and what protections consumers have.

**Cultural context.** The way Australians think about health insurance is different from how Americans think about it, because the systems are fundamentally different (Medicare vs. private-first). The way Kiwis approach travel is different from how Canadians do, because geography and seasonal patterns are different. Content that ignores these differences isn't just unhelpful — it's misleading.

**Local voice.** Content that reads naturally for that audience. Not British English awkwardly Americanised, not American English with a few spelling changes. Genuine voice that feels like it was written by someone who lives there — because it was.

## Why this is hard (and why most platforms don't do it)

Building truly local content for each market is expensive and slow. There's no shortcut. You can't generate it with AI and hope it's accurate — AI models default to their training data, which skews heavily toward US content. You can't outsource it to content farms — they produce volume, not quality. You can't template it — templates produce generic content that reads like templates.

Every country site we build requires genuine research into that market. What are the major retailers? What are the regulatory frameworks? What are the cultural norms around shopping, travel, and finance? What brands do people actually trust? What are the local payment methods, delivery expectations, and consumer rights?

This takes time. It takes expertise. And it doesn't scale the way Silicon Valley likes things to scale.

But that's exactly why it's valuable. Because the difficulty of doing this well is what creates the moat. Any platform can spin up a "localised" version of their site in a new country. Very few can build content that a local consumer would actually trust and find useful.

## The Five Eyes as a starting point

We chose to start with Five Eyes countries — Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, New Zealand, and Canada — for practical reasons. These are English-speaking markets with relatively similar (though far from identical) consumer cultures. Building for these markets first allows us to develop our approach to localisation without the added complexity of multilingual content.

But even within this group, the differences are striking. Australian consumers expect free returns as standard. American consumers expect loyalty programs and cashback. British consumers rely heavily on consumer rights legislation like the Consumer Rights Act 2015. New Zealand consumers deal with a smaller market and higher shipping costs. Canadian consumers navigate two official languages and cross-border shopping with the US.

These aren't edge cases. They're the core experience of consumers in each country. Getting them right is the difference between a useful platform and a generic one.

## Where AI fits in

AI is central to what we're building at Mubboo — but not in the way most people assume. We're not using AI to replace local expertise. We're using it to augment it.

AI helps us process and structure large volumes of product data, identify pricing trends, and keep information up to date across multiple markets simultaneously. It helps us spot patterns — like when a product category is trending in one market but not yet in another.

What AI doesn't do — and what we don't ask it to do — is replace the editorial judgment that comes from genuinely understanding a market. AI can tell you that a particular bank account has a 5.2% interest rate. It can't tell you that the bank has a reputation for poor customer service in that specific country, or that the rate comes with conditions that effectively make it inaccessible to most consumers.

That editorial layer — the human understanding of context, trust, and local reality — is what separates useful content from technically correct but practically useless content.

## The vision: 30+ countries

Our goal is to have Mubboo live in 30 or more countries within the next few years. That's ambitious, and it requires building systems and processes that can scale the local-first approach without sacrificing quality.

We're developing frameworks for how we research and build content for new markets. We're investing in tools that help us maintain accuracy across multiple countries simultaneously. And we're building relationships with local experts in each market who can validate that our content meets the bar.

The expansion into Europe will bring new challenges — multiple languages, different regulatory environments, different consumer expectations. But the core principle remains the same: every country deserves content built for its market, not translated from someone else's.

## Why this matters now

We're at an inflection point for consumer information online. AI is making it easier than ever to generate content — but it's also making it harder for consumers to know what they can trust. When anyone can produce a convincing-looking comparison article in seconds, the value of genuinely researched, locally expert content goes up, not down.

Consumers are becoming more discerning. They can tell when content is generic. They can tell when a platform doesn't really understand their market. And they're increasingly seeking out sources they can trust.

That's the opportunity we're building Mubboo for. Not to be the biggest platform, but to be the most trusted one in every market we serve. And trust, by definition, has to be earned locally.
