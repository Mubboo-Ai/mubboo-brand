The way we search for information is undergoing its biggest shift since the rise of mobile. For more than two decades, consumer search followed a predictable pattern: type keywords into a search engine, scan a page of blue links, click through to a website, and repeat. That model is breaking down.

AI assistants — from conversational chatbots to integrated browser copilots — are changing what consumers expect when they look for information. Instead of ten links and a sponsored ad, people increasingly want direct answers, tailored to their context.

## From keywords to conversations

The most visible change is in how queries are formed. Traditional search is keyword-driven: "best noise cancelling headphones 2026" or "cheap flights Sydney to Tokyo March." AI assistants encourage natural language: "What are the best headphones for long flights under $400?" or "Find me a cheap flight to Tokyo next month — I'm flexible on dates."

This shift matters because conversational queries carry more intent and context. A keyword search tells you what someone is looking for. A conversational query tells you what they're trying to accomplish.

## What this means for comparison platforms

For platforms that help consumers compare products, prices, and services, AI assistants represent both a threat and an opportunity.

The threat is disintermediation. If an AI assistant can directly answer "Which bank has the best savings rate in Australia right now?" then the consumer may never visit a comparison site at all.

The opportunity is in depth and trust. AI assistants are good at surface-level answers, but they struggle with nuance — regulatory differences between countries, hidden fee structures, seasonal pricing patterns, or the kind of local knowledge that comes from actually understanding a market.

## The local knowledge gap

This is where the biggest gap exists. Most AI models are trained on global data, which means their answers tend to default to US-centric information. Ask about the best mobile plan in New Zealand, and you might get recommendations that don't exist in that market. Ask about travel insurance regulations in the UK, and the answer might conflate US and UK rules.

Consumers don't just need answers — they need answers that are correct for their specific country, in their specific regulatory environment, at their specific price points.

## The trust question

There's also the question of trust. When an AI assistant recommends a product, is that recommendation based on what's genuinely best for the consumer, or on a commercial relationship? The lack of transparency in AI-generated recommendations is an issue that regulators are already beginning to examine.

Consumers deserve to know whether a recommendation is editorial or commercial. Platforms that are transparent about how they rank and recommend will have an advantage as AI-driven search becomes the norm.

## What comes next

The transition from keyword search to conversational AI won't happen overnight, and it won't be uniform. Different demographics, different countries, and different categories will adopt at different rates.

But the direction is clear. Consumers want faster, more personalised, more contextual answers. The platforms that can provide deep, accurate, locally relevant information — whether consumed directly or surfaced through AI — will be the ones that thrive.
