The buy now, pay later (BNPL) industry has grown from a niche payment option to a mainstream financial product used by millions of consumers across the English-speaking world. That growth has attracted regulatory scrutiny — and 2026 is shaping up to be the year those regulations start to bite.

Across Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, and New Zealand, regulators are moving to bring BNPL products under the same frameworks that govern traditional credit products. The changes are significant, and they'll affect both consumers and the companies that offer these services.

## Australia leads the charge

Australia has been at the forefront of BNPL regulation. The Treasury Laws Amendment Act, which came into effect in early 2026, formally classifies BNPL products as credit products under the National Consumer Credit Protection Act.

This means BNPL providers must now hold an Australian Credit Licence, conduct affordability assessments before approving transactions, and comply with responsible lending obligations. Providers must verify that a consumer can afford the repayments without substantial hardship — a requirement that was previously absent from the BNPL model.

The Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) has also introduced mandatory hardship provisions, requiring BNPL providers to offer repayment arrangements for consumers experiencing financial difficulty.

## The UK tightens the framework

The UK's Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) finalised its BNPL regulatory framework in late 2025, with full implementation rolling out through 2026. Under the new rules, BNPL providers must conduct creditworthiness assessments, provide clear and standardised terms, and give consumers access to the Financial Ombudsman Service for disputes.

One of the most impactful changes is the requirement for BNPL transactions to appear on credit reports. Previously, most BNPL usage was invisible to traditional credit scoring models, meaning consumers could accumulate significant BNPL debt without it affecting their credit profile — or being visible to other lenders assessing their capacity to repay.

## US moves toward federal oversight

In the United States, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has classified the largest BNPL providers as credit card issuers, subjecting them to the same regulatory requirements under the Truth in Lending Act. This includes standardised disclosures, dispute resolution rights, and refund protections.

Several states have also introduced their own BNPL-specific legislation, creating a patchwork of requirements that varies by jurisdiction. California and New York have been particularly active, introducing caps on late fees and mandatory cooling-off periods.

## New Zealand follows Australia's lead

New Zealand's Commerce Commission has proposed amendments to the Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act that would explicitly cover BNPL products. The proposed changes mirror much of Australia's approach, including mandatory affordability assessments and responsible lending obligations.

The timeline is slightly behind — full implementation is expected by mid-2027 — but the direction is clear.

## What this means for consumers

For consumers, the regulatory changes are broadly positive:

**Better protections.** Affordability checks mean fewer consumers will be approved for BNPL transactions they can't afford. Hardship provisions provide a safety net when circumstances change.

**More transparency.** Standardised disclosures make it easier to understand the true cost of BNPL, including late fees and penalties.

**Credit visibility.** BNPL appearing on credit reports is a double-edged sword — it can hurt consumers who've over-extended, but it also gives a more complete picture of financial health.

**Dispute resolution.** Access to ombudsman services and formal complaint processes gives consumers recourse when things go wrong.

## The industry response

BNPL providers have had mixed reactions. Some, particularly the larger players, have welcomed regulation as a way to legitimise the industry and differentiate from less scrupulous competitors. Others have warned that compliance costs could be passed on to consumers in the form of higher fees or reduced availability.

What's certain is that the era of unregulated BNPL is ending. For consumers, that's a good thing — provided they take the time to understand their rights under the new frameworks.
